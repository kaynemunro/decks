Three-year business case · Draft v1

Marketing Memory Layer
The brand operating system.

Operationalising the Memory Layer turns Let's Get Connected from a brand line into the underlying mechanism for how Marketing thinks, decides and acts — and recovers the cost of the platform many times over within the three-year window.

The programme is self-funding in its first year, returns $12.5M net over three — and the workforce benefit underpinning it is already actioned through the restructure.

The problem we're solving

AI is only as useful as the context it can work from. Today, brand truth, campaign learnings, customer signals and approved workflows sit fragmented across people, agencies and tools — so every cycle restarts from scratch, AI outputs drift, and the new operating model can't scale at the speed it needs to.

The future state we're enabling

A governed, always-on memory layer that every team, agent and partner draws from and contributes back to — so campaigns start from everything we already know, AI works inside guard rails, and a leaner operating model becomes faster and more accurate, not more exposed.

Benefits

Workforce optimisation is the dominant benefit, with media efficiency layered on top — and customer acquisition and churn benefits incremental from Year 2 onwards.

How we treat the workforce benefit in light of the recent restructure

The Product & Marketing leadership team directly built FTE benefits (and FTE investments) into the new structure on the basis that the Marketing Memory Layer would be deployed. The capability is part of what made the new operating model viable.

That said, we appreciate it's not as simple as one-for-one attribution. In practice, FTE reductions of this scale always rest on three things: an honest reassessment of what work is genuinely needed, a meaningful change in capability that reduces what humans have to do, and an acceptance that some delivery risk now sits with a leaner team. The MML is the middle lever — capability that makes a leaner operating model both sustainable and more effective, not just smaller.

This case takes the conservative middle position. We do not claim 100% of the confirmed FTE reduction value as MML-enabled — that would invite the fair challenge that the restructure was approved on its own merits. We also do not walk back to zero attribution — that would understate the dependency the new operating model has on the platform.

Instead, the case claims (a) the ~9 FTE worth of media and content production scope that McCann has absorbed at no additional cost because the platform exists between us and them, and (b) the additional 10 FTE in Year 2 unlocked by deeper automation maturity. The remaining in-house FTE reduction has not been claimed in this case — but to be clear, a large portion of that reduction is still enabled by this capability (e.g. Marketing leads, the insights function, design and experience, and creative roles). Full discussion in Q1 of the Q&A.

Incremental benefit — not claimed in FY1

Acquisition and churn benefits are deliberately held out of Year 1 and modelled from Year 2 onwards. The platform's predictive intelligence, frontline conversation flows and digital conversion monitoring all land in the second half of the build roadmap — and customer cohort tenure starts compounding from Year 2. Shown here so the reader sees the incremental upside in subsequent years.

Modelled conservatively at 1% incremental acquisition and 0.5% churn reduction across three product lines, valued on lifetime margin (monthly margin × tenure). Why these are credible numbers:

What drives the uplift

  • Faster, more accurate access to insight to action. Decision cycles compress from weeks to days and campaigns get to market faster as performance signal, synthetic profiles and prior learnings sit in a single queryable layer rather than scattered across teams, decks and tools — so the time between knowing and doing collapses, and results start landing sooner.
  • Faster, more timely conversation enhancement in the frontline. Retail save flows, contact centre saves and proactive outreach pull from live customer context — next-best-conversation, value-at-risk signal and tailored offers — instead of static playbooks updated monthly.
  • Better ability to monitor and address digital conversion. Real-time funnel signal, attribution and audience definitions feed both internal teams and the McCann media partner, so drop-off is detected and acted on inside the same cycle rather than the next reporting one.

Why 1% / 0.5% is the conservative number

  • 1% on acquisition = 3,500 net additional customers across pay monthly, prepaid and broadband — well below industry benchmarks for marketing analytics platforms (3–7% common).
  • 0.5% churn = ~1,750 saved customers — modelled at half the acquisition volume so the case doesn't double-count enthusiasm.
  • Acquisition and retention are driven by the same three mechanisms above, so any miss on one is partly compensated by the other.
  • Headline excludes store/channel performance (5–10% available), channel deployment resources and Finance/Commercial efficiency entirely.

Risks & opportunity

Risks

  • Capability-readiness window (largest risk): restructure effective 15 June 2026, MVP lands ~mid-August, full operational readiness ~mid-November. A 3–5 month gap exists where role reductions take effect ahead of the platform reaching full capability. Compressing the MVP timeline, or staging FTE exits to the platform milestones, is the primary mitigation.
  • Speed of decision on architecture, security and privacy positioning (One NZ + McCann) — the longest pole in the tent
  • Building the appropriate internal knowledge and expertise to operate the platform at scale
  • Fragmentation of systems, software and reporting across the business reducing the value the layer can extract

Opportunity

  • Operational readiness in ~6 months — full marketing function on the platform
  • 9 FTE optimised at Y1, +10 at Y2 — all redeployable to higher-value work
  • Brand-safe AI deployment by default across all rooms and agents
  • Decisions compound across cycles; speed and quality stop being a trade-off
  • Vendor consolidation reduces dependency and pricing exposure

Key questions for sign-off

Five questions to land before the case goes to the steering committee. Q1 is the most important — it determines how the workforce benefit is read.

Q1
How do we treat the FTE reduction that Marketing has recently confirmed, in light of the MML?

The Product & Marketing leadership team built FTE benefits (and investments) directly into the new structure on the basis that the MML would be deployed — capability and structure were planned together. A literal reading would still say "the FTE has gone, you can't claim it twice." The honest position is that the reduction reflects a combination: belief some resource wasn't needed, belief the capability changes what's possible, and accepted delivery risk on the remaining team — with the MML shoring up the middle of that combination. Three attribution options:

Option A — Full attribution

All ~28 net FTE plus the additional 10 Y2 FTE are claimed as MML-enabled. Rationale: the restructure was only signed off on the assumption the capability would land. Without the MML, leadership would likely be re-opening the restructure inside 6 months as the remaining team can't sustain the workload — at which point the cost of partial reversal exceeds the platform spend.

Pro: truthful to the leadership team's actual basis for the restructure. Con: hard to defend to the CFO as it can read as double-counting.

Option B — Zero attribution on confirmed FTE

The ~28 confirmed FTE is written off as restructure-attributable and not claimed. The case stands on the additional 10 Y2 FTE, media efficiency and the Y2 acquisition/churn upside only.

Pro: safest defensive position to the CFO. Con: materially weakens the case headline; understates the dependency the new operating model has on the platform.

Option C — Partial attribution (recommended)

Claim only (a) the ~9 FTE worth of media and content production scope that McCann has absorbed at no additional cost — defensible because the platform is what enables McCann to absorb that scope at that price, and (b) the additional 10 FTE in Y2 unlocked by deeper automation. We do not claim any of the ~28 in-house reductions confirmed through the restructure — those are attributed to the restructure itself, not this case.

Pro: defensible to the CFO ("we are claiming only what wouldn't have happened without the platform, not what was already booked"). Con: understates the operational dependency the new structure has on the platform — but that exposure can be raised separately as a delivery risk rather than a benefit claim.

Two options: (a) internal change capacity within the new MMI team, or (b) shared retainer with McCann for change & enablement support during the rollout window. Recommendation pending — internal capacity is constrained but cheaper; McCann is faster but adds run cost.
