Mechanics

How the Growth Fund works

A rep-level incentive that turns discount budget into a closing tool — earned by beating your own baseline, spent at the counter.

1

Start with $300

Every rep opens the trial with a $300 fund balance — real money they can deploy from day one.

2

Earn +$300 per sale above your own baseline

Each whole acquisition sale above the rep's own 4-week pre-trial baseline (drift-adjusted weekly) tops the fund up by $300. You compete with yourself, not the leaderboard.

3

Deploy as Phone Dollars at point of sale

The fund is spent at the counter as Phone Dollars to close deals that would otherwise walk.

Trial configuration

Two stores — Albany (10 active reps) and Sylvia Park (13 active reps). Started 8 Jun 2026, configured for 3 weeks to 28 Jun; activity and measurement continued into W4. Weeks are Monday-anchored; sales data runs to Fri 3 Jul, so W4 covers 5 of 7 days.

Deployments & data integrity

What was actually Growth Fund spend?

Reason codes only exist from 25 Jun. Before that, trial-rep loads mix Growth Fund deployments with legitimate business-as-usual service credits — competitor matches, remediation, complaint resolution. So we estimated the split, then validated the method.

How the BAU adjustment works

We took each rep's own Mar–early-Jun business-as-usual loading rate (~$2,300/wk across active reps) and subtracted it from their trial-week loads. What's left is the estimated GF-attributable spend. Validation: in W4, with codes live, actual non-GF loading was ~$2,974 against a BAU prediction of ~$2,288 — the method holds.

On the heavy loaders

Benjamin (~$1,081/wk), Umesh (~$1,053/wk), Jayson (~$467/wk), Kiran (~$343/wk) and Harmanjit (~$250/wk) are habitual BAU loaders. These are service credits — appropriate and expected, not misuse. The point is measurement: without codes we can't cleanly separate them from fund spend.

Return on investment

The honest answer is a range: 1.1× to 2.9×

Best estimate: $40.1k of CLV against $13.8k of GF-attributable spend ≈ 2.9×. Count every dollar trial reps loaded ($36.8k) as fund spend and it's still 1.1× — at or above break-even under the most hostile assumption.

Why show 6.4×? Dividing $40.1k by only the GF-coded loads (~$6.3k: $5,463 Sylvia Park + $800 Albany) reads ~6.4× — but W1–W2 predate reason codes, so their fund spend can't appear in a coded denominator. It flatters the result; use the 1.1×–2.9× range. W4's 0.7× is 5/7 days and post-window — treat as incomplete, not a trend.
Weekly ROI, best-estimate denominators

W1 ~3.6× · W2 negative (sales below baseline) · W3 ~2.8–15× (denominator uncertain; +19.6 incremental acqs ≈ $30.4k on ~$2.0k coded spend) · W4 ~0.7× (partial). The W3 peak is where the fund clearly paid.

The caveat that matters most

Point-of-sale GF tagging (growth_fund_boost_amount) never populated — closed-loop attribution never ran. All ROI here is inferred from incremental sales vs baseline. And ~2/3 of GF-matched loads sat next to upgrades or existing-customer sales, not new acquisitions — the fund was used as a general close-and-save tool. That's a policy decision we need to make, not a data error.

Store story · 2 of 2

Albany: an adoption gap, not a mechanism failure

Albany finished +0.9 incremental — flat. Only 3 of 10 active reps ever deployed the fund (3 coded loads, $800). Its W3 spike (+8) was offset by soft W1 and W4.

The evidence says the mechanic isn't the problem: where Albany reps did use the fund, it worked — Saad went from zero to seven acquisition sales. The fund simply never made it onto most of the floor.

Factually, for this audience: Albany's engagement was thin outside Saad, and several of the store's large loads were BAU service credits by leads — legitimate, but not fund activity.

What we learned

Five measurement gaps — every one fixable before the next cycle

1

No reason codes before 23 Jun

W1–W2 GF spend is a BAU-adjusted estimate — a validated method, but an estimate. A fair share of trial-rep loads were legitimate non-GF service credits.

Fix → mandatory GF reason code from day one
2

Ledger gap on Mon 22 Jun

The goodwill extract ends Sun 21 Jun and the system feed starts Tue 23 Jun — one day uncovered.

Fix → daily goodwill feed
3

Point-of-sale GF tagging never populated

No closed-loop attribution ran for the entire trial; all ROI is inferred from incremental sales vs baseline.

Fix → wire POS tagging before any scale decision
4

W4 is partial

Sales data covers 5 of 7 days in W4, and recent cohorts are still maturing — late-trial reads will firm up.

Fix → hold final read until the window matures
5

App baselines locked on ALL IFPs

The live page's baselines were locked on all IFPs (~4× acquisition-only), so it under-reports rep earnings until re-locked.

Fix → re-lock baselines acquisition-only
Recommendations

Continue, don't scale — and put a gate on the door

1

Run one clean, fully-measured cycle

4–6 weeks at the same two stores. Prove the number with proper instrumentation before spending more.

2

Fix measurement from day one

Mandatory GF reason code, POS tagging wired and tested, daily goodwill feed, baselines re-locked acquisition-only.

3

Make the upgrade-vs-acquisition policy call

~2/3 of the fund landed on non-acquisition closes (upgrades, existing-customer saves). Decide whether that's in scope and priced in — or out of bounds — before the next cycle.

4

Fix Albany adoption with the Sylvia Park playbook

Floor-lead sponsorship and a daily scorecard before adding stores. No movement in 2 weeks → swap in a contrast store.

5

Add light netting or a store-level gate on payouts

Floored per-rep payouts ($18.3k) ran ~2.3× the ~$8k store-level net lift. Keep the individual incentive; stop paying for offsetting noise.

★

Decision gate

If the clean cycle delivers ROI ≥ 3× with proper tagging, scale to 5–6 stores. If it doesn't, we'll know exactly why — because this time we'll have measured it.
